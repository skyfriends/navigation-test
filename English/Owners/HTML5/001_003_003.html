3.3 Extruder Overview Screen

3.3.1 Section Overview

This section discusses the Extruder Overview screen. The operation of most of the extruder system devices and conditions can be monitored and controlled from this screen. For example, most of the extruder system devices with electrical drives can be monitored and controlled from this screen. Many of the extruder system fluids flows can be monitored and controlled from this screen. The operation of the various components that make up the dry feed system are monitored and controlled from this screen. To make the details of the Extruder Overview screen easier to grasped, the screen is explained by dividing the screen down into a handful of functional areas:

  • Feeder Section
  • Preconditioner Section
  • Extruder Section
  • Knife Section
  • Other

The Extruder Overview screen is accessed by clicking the “(F1) Extruder Overview” button in the region of the Main screen with the forest green background color. It can also be accessed from any screen by pressing the F1 function key on the APM console keyboard.

3.3.2 Entire Screen

An example of the Extruder Overview screen after the extruder system has successfully completed startup is reproduced inFigure 3.32. The green colors indicate devices that are currently in Running states. The flow of material through the extruder system progresses from left to right. The customer is responsible for always maintaining a sufficient level of dry feed material in the Surge Bin (1) when the extruder system is in operation. The amount of dry feed material in the Live Bin (2) is maintained by gravity flow of dry feed to the live bin from the surge bin through a two-position gate called the Slide Gate (3) when the mass of dry feed in the live bin drops below a minimum weight. The Screw Feeder (4) transfers dry feed from the live bin to the Preconditioner (5) at a constant mass rate that is specified by the customer. In the preconditioner, the dry feed is mixed with Preconditioner Process Water (6), Preconditioner Process Steam (7), and any customer-specific liquids (e.g., oils, dyes, flavorings) that are required by the customer's recipes to formulate the product. (Note that the flow of customer-specific liquids is not shown on this screen, but only on the liquid screens.) The feed mixture then flows into the extruder through the two-position gate called the Downspout (8). The feed mixture is cooked as both thermal and mechanical energy is added to it as it travels under high pressure through the Extruder Barrel (9). The thermal energy is added by maintaining elevated temperatures on the zones of the barrel (discussed in See Section 3.4, Extruder Barrel Temperatures Screen.). The mechanical energy is added by maintaining high pressures inside the extruder barrel, in part with the design of the extruder barrel internal screw, and in part with the Back Pressure Valve (BPV) (12). In addition, while the feed mixture is being cooked inside the extruder barrel, Extruder Process Water (10) and Extruder Process Steam (11) is added to the feed mixture as called for by the product recipe. As the cooked raw product is forced out through the end of the extruder barrel and shaped by the extruder dies, it is sliced into kibbles with a Knife (13) rotating at high speeds. These product kibbles are then pulled through the Knife Hood (14) to equipment downstream of the extruder system via a negative pressure pneumatic product transfer system. An Indicator (15) of the status of the downstream processing equipment, whether it is ready to receive raw product from the extruder system, is provided.

On the top of the Extruder Overview screen are some objects that will be discussed in detail See Section 3.3.5, Extruder Section.. There are two Buttons (16) and (17) that bring forward faceplatees that provide options for extruder system control such as starting, stopping and flushing the extruder system. There is a Button (18) that commands all extruder system devices to their Auto control modes. There is a Faceplate (19) that provides status and control options for operation of the dry feed system. And there is a faceplate (20) that displays dry feed totals and energy statistics.

At the bottom of the Extruder Overview screen is a bank of Buttons (21) that allow the operator to quickly change to another of the most commonly used screens

3.3.3.1 Gravimetric Feed Mode

When the feeder section is being operated in gravimetric mode, the level of dry feed material in the live bin is determined from the input from the live bin load cells which measure the weight of the material inside it. The volume of dry feed material in the live bin is calculated by applying the known dry feed density specified as a parameter in the currently loaded formula. Finally, the level of dry feed material in the live bin is calculated by applying the known volume and dimensions of the cylindrically-shaped live bin. The two live bin level sensors indicate when the level of dry feed material in the live bin is above and below each level, respectively.

The PLC issues an output signal to open the surge bin slide gate to fill the live bin when the live bin weight drops below a predetermined value. The PLC monitors the weight increase in the live bin as the live bin is refilling. The PLC issues an output to close the surge bin slide gate when the live bin weight reaches a predetermined value. Note that the live bin will always fill to a weight slightly greater than the weight value calculated to close the surge bin slide gate because of the in-transit inventory of material between the surge bin slide gate and the material level in the live bin.

3.3.3.2 Volumetric Feed Mode

When the feeder section is being operated in volumetric mode, the live bin is refilled based on input from the live bin high level sensor. If the live bin high level sensor input signal is false for a period of time that is calculated from the volumetric feed rate setpoint, the live bin capacity, and the formula bulk density, the PLC issues an output signal to open the surge bin slide gate to refill the live bin. The surge bin slide gate is opened and the live bin is filled until the high-level sensor is reached.

3.3.4 Preconditioner Section

The preconditioner section is where dry feed material is mixed with process steam, process water, and any customer-specific liquids (e.g., oils, dyes, flavorings) before the mixture flows down to the extruder inlet through the downspout (see Figure 3.37). This section includes the preconditioner, the downspout, and the equipment necessary for controlling the flow of preconditioner process water and process steam.

Wenger provides three varieties of preconditioners:

  • Differential Diameter Conditioner (DDC)
  • High Intensity Preconditioner (HIP)
  • High Shear Conditioner (HSC)

The DDC features two mixer shafts. These mixer shafts rotate at different speeds but are driven by a single constant-speed motor (one shaft rotates at the motor's speed while the other is linked to the motor through a fixed-ratio gear reducer). The two DDC mixer shafts rotate at fixed speeds that the operator cannot change. When the DDC is in the Auto control mode the PLC always determines the rotation directions of the two shafts. But the operator can override the PLC and select the direction of rotation (forward or reverse) for the two mixer shafts by setting the DDC to Hand control mode (clicking the DDC HOA button (1) in Figure 3.37), and then clicking either the Forward or Reverse direction buttons on the DDC HOA faceplate in Figure 3.38.

The HIP features two mixer shafts. Each of these two mixer shafts is driven by its own dedicated motor. The speed that each mixer shaft rotates is independent of the speed of the other shaft, and the rotational speed of each mixer shaft is selectable by the operator. Similarly, the direction that each mixer shaft rotates is independent of the direction of the other shaft, and the direction of each mixer shaft is selectable by the operator. The speed and direction of each mixer shaft can be specified by the operator in Hand control mode via an HOA faceplate, and can be specified by formula parameters in Auto control mode.

The HSC features a single mixer shaft that rotates in the forward direction only, but the speed that the mixer shaft rotates is selectable by the operator in Hand control mode via an HOA faceplate, and can be specified by a formula parameter in Auto control mode.

From this point on in this document only the operation of the DDC type of preconditioner will be discussed.

The preconditioner process water and process steam flow rates each depend in Auto control mode on the recipe for the specific product being formulated, and are each usually specified as a percentage of the dry feed rate. When the process water PID control loop is in the Auto control mode the PLC always determines the process water flow rate. The operator can change the process water Auto control mode flow rate setpoint by clicking the Water PID button (2) in Figure 3.37), and then changing the value in the setpoint text box (4) in the Cylinder Water PID faceplate in Figure 3.39. Changing the process steam setpoint is done in the same fashion. The operator can also override the PLC and set the process water flow control valve to a specific position by clicking the Water PID button (2) in Figure 3.37), setting the process water flow to Manual control mode and then changing the value in the percent output text box (5) of the Cylinder Water PID faceplate in Figure 3.39. The same holds for changing the process steam flow control valve position.

3.3.5 Extruder Section

The extruder section is where the feed mixture is cooked under high pressure. This section includes the extruder, the lube pump, the BPV, and the process water and process steam that are injected into the extruder barrel (not to be confused with the water and steam that are circulated through the extruder barrel jackets and used to control the temperatures of the various extruder barrel temperature zones). Figure 3.41 reproduces the region of the Extruder Overview screen where the extruder section can be seen.

The extruder motor only rotates in the forward direction. Its speed is usually specified in RPM as a formula item in Auto control mode that depends on the recipe for whatever product is being produced. When the extruder is in the Auto control mode the PLC always determines the extruder speed. But the operator can override the PLC and specify the extruder speed by setting the extruder to Hand control mode (clicking the Extruder HOA button (1) in Figure 3.41), and then changing the value in the manual setpoint text box (10) on the Extruder HOA faceplate in Figure 3.43.

The lube pump is a fixed speed device that circulates lubrication oil through the extruder motor bearing housing. The lube pump must be running before the extruder motor can be started. When the lube pump is in the Auto control mode the PLC always determines whether the lube pump is running or stopped. But the operator can override the PLC and control the lube pump by clicking the Lube Pump HOA button (2) in Figure 3.41, and then clicking either Hand (4) or Off (5) buttons on the Lube Pump HOA faceplate in Figure 3.44.

The extruder process water and process steam flow rates each depend in Auto control mode on the recipe for the specific product being formulated, and each are usually specified as a percentage of the dry feed rate. When the extruder process water PID control loop is in the Auto control mode the PLC always determines the process water flow rate. The operator can change the process water Auto control mode flow rate setpoint by clicking the Water PID button (4) in Figure 3.41), and then changing the value in the setpoint text box (4) on the Extruder Water PID faceplate in Figure 3.45. Changing the extruder process steam setpoint is done in the same fashion. The operator can also override the PLC and set the extruder process water flow control valve to a specific position by clicking the Water HOA button (4) in Figure 3.41), setting the process water PID control loop to Manual control mode and then changing the value in the percent output text box (5) of the Extruder Water PID faceplate in Figure 3.41. The same holds for changing the extruder process steam flow control valve position.

The BPV is a valve that has a single inlet and two outlets. One outlet, called the process outlet, channels raw product material from the extruder through a shaping die and then on to the knife. The other outlet, called the reject outlet, channels material down through an opening at the bottom of the BPV and out of the extruder system (where a container should be in place to collect the rejected raw product). The BPV can be controlled in either of three states: Process, Reject and Die Relief. When the BPV is commanded to the Process state the BPV allows raw product to flow from the extruder through the valve's process outlet, the size of which can be enlarged or restricted to maintain a desired pressure at the end of the extruder. When the BPV is commanded to the Reject state the BPV allows raw product to flow from the extruder through the valve's reject outlet. When the BPV is commanded to the Die Relief state the BPV allows raw product to flow from the extruder to both the process outlet and reject outlet. In addition, the size of the process outlet opening is set to its maximum. When the BPV is in the Auto control mode the PLC always determines whether the BPV is in the Process, Reject or Die Relief control mode. But the operator can override the PLC and control the BPV by clicking the BPV HOA button (3) in Figure 3.41, and then clicking either Hand (4) or Off (5) buttons in the BPV HOA faceplate in Figure 3.46.

3.3.6 Knife Section

The knife section is where the raw product that is forced under pressure out of the extruder section through shaping die is then cut to length and conveyed out of the extruder system. This section includes the knife and the knife hood. Figure 3.47 reproduces the region of the Extruder Overview screen where the knife section can be seen.

The knife speed is usually specified in RPM as a formula item that depends on the recipe for whatever product is being produced. The knife hood is a channel or ductwork through which the product is pneumatically conveyed to equipment downstream of the extruder system, usually a dryer, for further processing. Typically, there is a product transfer fan and airlock downstream of the extruder system that, when the knife hood diverter is open, creates a pressure drop sufficient to pull the product through the knife hood and over to the downstream equipment. This negative pressure is not exerted on the knife hood when the knife hood diverter is closed. In this case, any raw product exiting the extruder and cut by the knife will fall out through the bottom of the knife hood. (The product transfer fan and airlock are sometimes provided as the last section of the extruder system, but they are more typically the first section of a dryer system, and so they won't be addressed in this document.)

  1. Knife Status/Control - Displays whether the knife is running or stopped. Also, provides a button with which the knife HOA faceplate in Figure 3.50, can be displayed.
  2. Knife Hood Status/Control - Displays whether the knife hood gate is open or closed. Also, provides a button with which the knife hood HOA faceplate in Figure 3.51, can be displayed.
  3. Knife Speed Indicator - Displays the knife speed in RPM.

When the extruder system startup sequence is being executed by the PLC, at some point in the startup sequence, just prior to when the knife is to be started, the PLC will prompt the operator to confirm that the knife is safe to start by displaying a button (1) as in Figure 3.48. The PLC will not start the knife until the operator has clicked this button to confirm that the knife is safe to start.

When the extruder system startup sequence is being executed by the PLC, at some point in the startup sequence the PLC will determine that conditions will be such that it is ok for the knife hood diverter to be changed from the Reject position to the Process position. It will be at this point that the PLC will prompt the operator to command the knife hood diverter position change by displaying a button (1) as in Figure 3.49. The PLC will wait until the operator has clicked this button to change the knife hood diverter position from Reject to Process.

The Extruder Commands faceplate is shown in Figure 3.55. It features a group of buttons with which the operator can instruct the PLC to supervise and handle the details of starting up and shutting down the extruder system. As an example, when the operator clicks the Start Extruder button the PLC will be instructed to execute a normal startup of the extruder system. This is a sequence of steps, starting with ensuring that the surge bin is filled with dry feed material and ending with opening the knife hood diverter to allow raw product to be conveyed to processing equipment downstream of the extruder system. The normal startup sequence ensures that each motor is started up at the proper time, that the flow of water and steam begin flowing at the proper time and at the specified rates, that the extruder barrel zones are controlled at the specified temperatures, and that the downspout, BPV and knife hood gates are positioned when the necessary conditions are satisfied. When any abnormal conditions are encountered during the sequence a corresponding operator alarm is triggered. These automated sequences allow for optimal, consistent, repeatable extruder system operation, minimize human error, and relieve operators of the responsibility of executing the required sequence steps. All PLC supervised startup and shutdown sequences are described in detail in General Information.
